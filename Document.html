MY FAVOURITE PEOPLE


MR. RAM PARSAD YADAV: A Story of Determination

My father was born on May 5, 1985, in Hisaamuddin Pur, a small village in Ayodhya. He completed his education up to the 12th grade. Throughout his life, he faced many challenges and hardships, but he never allowed these obstacles to defeat him. In 2002, he got married and soon after moved to Yamunanagar district in Haryana, hoping to build a better career. Despite his relentless efforts, success did not come easily. He encountered many setbacks and disappointments, but he never lost hope or gave up. Driven by the desire to provide a good education for his children, my father worked tirelessly, often sacrificing his own comfort for our future. Over time, his hard work began to yield results. Slowly but surely, he started to achieve success. He was able to buy a tractor and expand his work, marking a significant milestone in his journey. Even today, my father continues to work with unwavering dedication to ensure his children have the best opportunities in life. His perseverance, resilience, and commitment to his family are truly inspiring. My father is my role model, and his story motivates me every day to work hard and never give up.

FATHER OF NATION : BAPU

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi was a key leader in India’s struggle for independence from British rule. Born in 1869, Gandhi trained as a lawyer but became renowned for his philosophy of nonviolent resistance, or Satyagraha. His approach to protest—using peaceful civil disobedience rather than violence—became a powerful tool in the fight for civil rights and freedom, not just in India but around the world. Gandhi led several major movements, including the Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–22), the Salt March (1930), and the Quit India Movement (1942), each mobilizing millions of Indians to peacefully oppose British laws and policies. He also worked tirelessly for social reforms, campaigning against the caste system and untouchability, and advocating for the rights of marginalized communities and women. Beyond politics, Gandhi promoted self-reliance through the Swadeshi movement, urging people to use local products and support rural industries. He believed in simplicity, truth, and living in harmony with nature. Gandhi’s commitment to nonviolence and justice inspired global leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela. Even after India’s independence in 1947, Gandhi continued to work for communal harmony until his assassination in 1948. His legacy of peace, nonviolence, and social justice continues to inspire people worldwide

RATAN NAVAL TATA

Ratan Tata is widely regarded as one of the most inspirational business leaders of our time. As the former chairman of Tata Sons, he transformed the Tata Group into a global powerhouse, expanding its presence in more than 100 countries. What sets Ratan Tata apart is not just his exceptional business acumen, but his unwavering commitment to ethics, integrity, and social responsibility. Under his leadership, Tata Motors acquired Jaguar Land Rover, and Tata Steel acquired Corus, marking India’s emergence on the world stage. Ratan Tata’s humility and simplicity are legendary. Despite his immense success, he remains approachable and grounded, often interacting directly with employees at all levels. He is known for his philanthropic efforts, with a significant portion of Tata Group’s profits directed towards education, healthcare, and rural development through the Tata Trusts. His vision goes beyond profits; he believes in building businesses that improve lives and contribute to society. Ratan Tata’s ability to lead with compassion, make bold decisions, and maintain the highest ethical standards makes him a role model for aspiring entrepreneurs and leaders. His life teaches us that true success is not just measured by wealth or power, but by the positive impact one creates in the world.

MISSILE MAN OF INDIA

Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam (Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam) was a renowned Indian scientist and the 11th President of India, often called the “Missile Man of India” for his pivotal role in developing the country’s missile and nuclear weapons programs. Born on 15 October 1931 in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, into a humble family, Kalam’s early life was marked by simplicity and hard work. Despite financial challenges, he excelled academically, earning a degree in physics from Saint Joseph’s College and later specializing in aeronautical engineering at the Madras Institute of Technology. Kalam began his career at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) in 1958 and later moved to the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), where he directed the launch of India’s first indigenous Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III), successfully placing the Rohini satellite in orbit in 1980. He was instrumental in the development of the Agni and Prithvi missiles and played a key role in the Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, which established India as a nuclear power. Elected President in 2002, Kalam was widely loved as the “People’s President” for his humility, vision, and dedication to youth and education. He advocated for a developed India through his “Vision 2020” and inspired millions with his writings and speeches. Dr. Kalam passed away on 27 July 2015, leaving behind a legacy of scientific excellence, patriotism, and inspiration

SACHIN TENDULKAR

Sachin Tendulkar, often called the "God of Cricket," is widely regarded as one of the greatest cricketers in history. Born on April 24, 1973, in Mumbai, India, Tendulkar showed immense talent from a young age and made his debut for the Indian national team at just 16. Over a career that spanned more than two decades, he set numerous records, including being the first player to score 100 international centuries and the highest run-scorer in both Test and One-Day International (ODI) cricket, amassing over 34,000 runs in total. Tendulkar’s batting prowess, consistency, and ability to perform under pressure made him a global icon and a role model for aspiring cricketers. He was the first cricketer to score a double century in ODIs and played 200 Test matches, a record unmatched by any other player. His humility and sportsmanship further endeared him to fans worldwide. After retiring from international cricket in 2013, Tendulkar was honored with the Bharat Ratna, India’s highest civilian award, recognizing his immense contribution to the sport and the nation. He remains a beloved figure in cricket, inspiring millions with his dedication, perseverance, and legendary achievements.

MOTHER TERESA

Mother Teresa, born Anjezë Gonxhe Bojaxhiu on August 26, 1910, in Skopje, was a Roman Catholic nun renowned for her lifelong dedication to serving the poorest and most vulnerable. She moved to India in her youth, where she founded the Missionaries of Charity in 1950, an organization devoted to providing “wholehearted free service to the poorest of the poor.” Under her leadership, the Missionaries of Charity grew to operate in over 130 countries, running homes for the dying, orphanages, leprosy centers, and soup kitchens. Mother Teresa’s humanitarian work was marked by compassion and a commitment to dignity for all, regardless of background or faith. She opened the first Home for the Dying, Nirmal Hriday, in Kolkata, and established Shanti Nagar for leprosy patients, as well as homes for abandoned children. Her efforts to promote interfaith harmony and peace earned her international acclaim, including the Nobel Peace Prize in 1979 and India’s highest civilian honor, the Bharat Ratna. Despite facing criticism for her views and the conditions in some of her homes, Mother Teresa’s legacy as a symbol of selfless service endures. She was canonized as Saint Teresa of Calcutta in 2016, and her life continues to inspire millions worldwide
